What is a sewer camera inspection? (And when you actually need one.)

A small waterproof camera goes down the line and you watch the live feed. It is the only way to diagnose a sewer problem without guessing. Here is how it works, when it is worth it, and when it is not.

JR
J. Reimer
Lead tech · Winnipeg Sewer & Drain

Most plumbing problems happen inside a pipe you can't see. A drain backs up, a toilet gurgles, water pools in the yard. Without a camera, every diagnosis is a guess based on symptoms.

A sewer camera inspection takes the guesswork out. Here's how it works, when it makes sense, and when it doesn't.

The short version
A small waterproof camera travels through your sewer line on a flexible cable while you watch the live feed on a screen. It's the only way to diagnose a sewer problem accurately. The inspection takes about an hour, and at WSD it's the first step on every job.

How a sewer camera inspection works

A waterproof camera head — about the size of a hockey puck, sometimes smaller for residential lines — sits on the end of a flexible push cable. The tech feeds it through an existing cleanout or a removable fixture into your sewer line.

The head has a bright LED ring around the lens. As it travels down the pipe, you watch the live feed on a monitor. The camera runs the full length of the line, from inside the house all the way out to where it connects to the city main.

The cable carries a built-in transmitter, so a locator above ground can pinpoint the exact position and depth of the camera. If we spot a problem, we mark the spot on the surface — so any digging or repair is done in exactly the right place, not by trial and error.

What you actually see

A camera inspection shows:

  • The condition of the pipe — cracks, breaks, corrosion, offset joints
  • The pipe material — clay tile, cast iron, concrete, ABS, PVC
  • Tree root intrusion, and how bad it is
  • Grease and buildup on the pipe walls
  • Foreign objects stuck in the line
  • Sags or "bellies" where water and waste collect
  • Improper slope or alignment
  • The connection to the city main

You don't have to take anyone's word for what's wrong. You see it yourself, on the screen, while it's happening.

When you should get one

Five situations where it's worth doing.

  • Before buying an older home. A pre-purchase scope on any home built before 1980 catches sewer issues before you take ownership. Replacement on an older line can run more than $10,000 — knowing before closing is leverage.
  • Recurring drain clogs. A drain that backs up every few months has a cause. A camera finds it. Without one, you're paying to clear the same clog over and over.
  • Slow drains across multiple fixtures. When several drains run slow at once, the problem is in the main line, not the fixtures. A camera tells you where.
  • After a major sewer event. A backup, a frozen line, or a root removal should always end with a camera to confirm the line is clear and the pipe wasn't damaged.
  • Before any repair quote. No quote is honest without a camera. A price quoted before an inspection is a guess — and a guess is how repair jobs double halfway through.

When you don't need one

A first-time clog in a single fixture — one slow bathroom sink, one toilet that backed up once — usually doesn't need a camera. Standard drain cleaning is the right first step. If the problem comes back, then a camera makes sense.

What a WSD camera inspection includes

  • A full run from inside the house out to the city main
  • Live footage on screen, with you watching
  • A digital recording of the inspection for your records
  • A written report of anything we find
  • A flat quote for any recommended work

You don't have to commit to repairs based on the inspection. You get the footage, the report, and the quote — then you decide. See the full camera inspection service, or book one below.

Frequently asked questions

How much does a sewer camera inspection cost in Winnipeg?

We don't post a flat price, because the scope is usually rolled into the work it informs. Ask the dispatcher and you'll have the number before we book — and on most jobs, the inspection pays for itself by avoiding a guess-based repair quote.

How long does a sewer camera inspection take?

Usually 30 to 60 minutes. Longer lines, or lines with obstructions, take more time. Most residential inspections fit inside a one-hour appointment.

Can a sewer camera see everything?

A camera navigates most lines from 1.5 inches up to 8 inches in diameter. It can't see through a completely blocked line — cleaning has to happen first — or through a fully submerged section. In most situations, a skilled tech gets useful footage of the entire line.

Do I need a camera inspection if I'm buying a home?

For any home built before 1980, yes. Sewer line replacement on an older home can cost more than $10,000. A pre-purchase inspection is cheap insurance.

What's the difference between a sewer camera and a drain camera?

Same tool, different scale. A small drain camera handles 1.5 to 2 inch lines — sink, tub, washing-machine drains. A sewer camera handles 3 inch and larger lines — the main sewer. Most service trucks carry both.
