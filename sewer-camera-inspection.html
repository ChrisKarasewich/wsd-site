When you need it

When the line keeps speaking up — or you're about to sign for one.

Most sewer problems are not what you'd guess from upstairs. A "kitchen clog" turns out to be 60 feet of grease in the main. A "bad smell" turns out to be a cracked tile under the slab. Cabling without a camera is paying someone to guess.

The camera replaces guessing with footage. You see exactly what's in the line — roots, scale, broken pipe, foreign object — and where it is, to within 12 inches of the surface. The quote follows the picture.

The route

What the camera sees, from the cleanout to the city main.

SELF-LEVELING HD HEAD

The camera always shows the floor of the pipe at the bottom of the screen, even on a roll. Same orientation you'd have if you crawled in.

DISTANCE COUNTER, LIVE

A footage counter on the reel tells you exactly how far from the cleanout each finding is. We mark it on screen as we go.

SONDE LOCATOR ABOVE GROUND

A radio transmitter in the camera head pings to a locator wand on the lawn. Marks the spot for a repair within 12 inches.

Equipment

Different line, different camera.

Mainline crawler

3″ to 6″ lines.

Sewer laterals, building drains, mainlines. 200ft push reel, self-leveling HD head, integrated sonde for above-ground location. The everyday camera.

Mini push-rod

1.5″ to 3″ lines.

Kitchen branches, lavatory drains, washing-machine standpipes. Slim flexible rod gets around the tight P-traps and 90-degree elbows the mainline camera can't.

Sonde locator

Above-ground location.

512Hz transmitter in every camera head. The handheld locator wand finds the camera through up to 20 feet of dirt and concrete, marks the spot with paint, gives you the depth.

What the footage shows

Eight findings the camera turns into a written report.

01

Root intrusion.

Hair-thin roots growing in through a clay tile joint, or thick woody masses wrapped around the pipe. The camera shows you exactly which joint and which species.

Next → Cable or jet, then root-control foam.
02

Belly (sag) in the pipe.

Standing water that doesn't drain because the pipe has settled into a low spot. Debris accumulates here first — the source of repeat clogs.

Next → Spot repair to re-grade that section.
03

Offset or separated joint.

Two sections of pipe no longer aligned — soil washes in, roots find the gap. Common on Winnipeg clay tile near the city main connection.

Next → Spot repair or trenchless liner.
04

Broken, cracked, or collapsed pipe.

Hairline crack, longitudinal fracture, or full structural failure. The camera footage drives the repair decision: liner vs. dig.

Next → Liner or excavation, with locate marks.
05

Grease, scale, and soft buildup.

Wall coating that reduces pipe diameter and starts the next clog. Cast iron mains over 50 years old almost always have it.

Next → Hydro jetting or chain-flail descaling.
06

Foreign objects.

Toothbrushes, "flushable" wipes, kids' toys, lost rings. We find them, mark the location, and recommend a retrieval method.

Next → Mechanical retrieval, no demo work.
07

Soil intrusion.

Dirt and gravel washing into the pipe through a crack or open joint. A leading indicator that the line is failing structurally.

Next → Excavate and replace that section.
08

Improper or illegal connections.

Sump pump or weeping tile illegally tied into the sanitary lateral. A common reason for basement backups during heavy rain.

Next → Re-route to storm or sump drainage.

Camera inspection FAQ

Questions before we run the camera.

How long does a camera inspection take?
A residential lateral — house to city main — takes about 45 minutes to 90 minutes start to finish, including setup, the camera run, sonde location of any findings, and walking you through the footage on a tablet before we leave. Mini push-rod inspections on a single branch line are faster — usually under an hour.
Do you need a cleanout? What if I don't have one?
A proper main cleanout is the best entry point — it gives the camera straight-line access and saves time. If you don't have one, we can sometimes go through a pulled toilet or a roof vent stack, but it's slower and a bit messier. We'll tell you on the phone whether your house likely has a cleanout based on the era it was built. If you don't have one, we can quote installing one — it pays for itself the next time anything goes wrong.
Will the camera fit in my line?
The mainline camera fits any pipe 3 inches or larger — which covers every residential sewer lateral and most building drains in Winnipeg. For smaller branch lines (kitchen sinks, lavatories, washing machine standpipes) we switch to a mini push-rod camera that goes down to 1.5 inch. The only lines we can't scope are pressurized water supply lines (different game) and 1 1/4″ trap arms.
What if the camera finds nothing wrong?
Great — that's the best outcome of a camera inspection. You get the footage, a one-page report saying the line is sound, and a recommended next-scope date (usually 5 to 7 years out for an older Winnipeg property). Lots of our jobs end this way. We don't invent problems.
Can I get a copy of the footage for an insurance claim?
Yes — every camera job comes with the full video file, emailed to you the same day, plus a written one-page report with timestamps. Insurance adjusters and city public-works departments accept it. We've also been called as a witness in a couple of disputes; if you need formal documentation beyond the standard report, just ask.
Why is camera inspection so important for older Winnipeg homes?
A lot of homes built before about 1970 still have clay tile or Orangeburg laterals between the house and the city main. Both materials fail predictably with age — roots get in through joint seals, the pipe sags or collapses, soil washes in. The footage is the only reliable way to know whether you've got 20 years left on the line or two.
Does the camera fee really roll into the repair if I book one?
Yes. If we camera the line, find a problem, and you book us to fix it within 30 days, the camera fee comes off the repair invoice. If the camera is the whole job — insurance documentation, peace of mind on an older line — the camera fee is what you pay, flat.
