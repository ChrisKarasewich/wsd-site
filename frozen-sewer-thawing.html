When you need it

Nothing's draining — and the cable can't get past 18 feet.

A frozen sewer doesn't act like a clog. Every drain in the house is dead at once. The toilet won't flush, the floor drain backs up the moment you run a sink upstairs, and a cable run hits something hard and slick around the property line and won't go further. That's an ice plug.

In Winnipeg this is a January-to-March job. Frost goes 7 to 9 feet deep, sewer laterals are buried 6 to 10, and any spot where standing water sits in the pipe overnight is where the freeze starts. Steam is the only safe way out. Don't pour boiling water down the cleanout. Don't run a torch in a basement. Don't trust anything with the word "salt" in its instructions.

Cold snap, then nothing drains
A run of −30°C nights, then suddenly every fixture in the house is slow or backed up. Classic frozen lateral.
Cable hits a hard, slick wall
A drain auger goes in 15 to 25 feet, hits something glassy and slips back. Cable can't cut ice — it just polishes it.
Seasonal cabin or empty house
A property sat unheated for a week. The trap-arm to the main froze first; everything behind it followed.
City main froze and backed up
Rare, but it happens. We thaw from your cleanout outward; the city handles their side past the property line.
How steam thawing works

A live-steam line walks the ice back to water, foot by foot.

TRUCK-MOUNTED BOILER

A diesel-fired boiler in the van produces 8 gallons per minute of 212°F steam, run through an insulated copper feed line. The hose stays warm even at −35°C.

WALK-THE-ICE TECHNIQUE

The steam tip pushes against the plug face. Ice turns to water, the rod advances 6 to 12 inches, water flows back. Repeat until the rod hits the city main or daylight.

SAFE ON EVERY PIPE MATERIAL

Live steam runs at the same temperature in a clay-tile lateral, a cast-iron drop, or a PVC service. No torch heat, no electric arc, no risk of fire or pipe damage.

Methods compared

Why steam — and why not the alternatives.

Steam thaw

What we run.

212°F live steam through an insulated rod. Walks the ice plug back to water. Safe on clay, cast iron, PVC, ABS, concrete.

WSD METHOD
Hot-water flush

Sometimes a backup.

A jet line running pre-heated water at low pressure. Works on short surface freezes inside the basement, slow on a long buried lateral.

BACKUP OPTION
Electric arc thaw

Banned on plastic.

Runs current through the pipe to heat it. Used on cast-iron mains for decades. Will not work on PVC or clay, and burned down houses in the 1980s when it sparked a leak.

WSD WON'T USE
Torch / open flame

House fire risk.

A plumber's torch on a frozen drain inside a basement is how a sewer thaw becomes a kitchen fire. Disqualifying. Don't let anyone do this.

Before we arrive

Things to do — and things to absolutely not.

A frozen sewer doesn't get worse by waiting an hour for a truck. It gets a lot worse if you try to fix it yourself with whatever's in the garage. Here's the playbook we walk every caller through on the phone.

DO
  • Stop running water.

    Don't flush, don't shower, don't do laundry. Every gallon you put in has nowhere to go and ends up on the floor.

  • Find your main cleanout.

    Usually in the basement near the foundation wall, capped with a brass or black plastic plug. We come in there.

  • Heat the basement to 18°C+.

    Warmer ambient air helps the thaw and keeps the steamer running clean. Open the utility-room door.

  • Note when it started.

    "Stopped draining around 6 a.m. after a −34°C night" tells us roughly where the plug is before we get there.

  • Clear access.

    Move the storage shelf, the freezer chest, the bikes. We need a 4-foot working radius around the cleanout.

DON'T
  • Don't pour boiling water down the cleanout.

    You'll melt a tiny bowl in the front of the ice plug, then 8 cubic feet of hot water comes back at you all over the basement floor.

  • Don't use rock salt or chemical thaw.

    Calcium-chloride pellets ruin clay tile mortar joints, eat at cast iron, and most of them never reach the ice plug anyway.

  • Don't use a propane torch.

    Open flame on a frozen drain in an enclosed basement is a house-fire waiting to happen. Disqualifying for any plumber who suggests it.

  • Don't rent an electric thaw machine.

    Two cables clipped to the pipe, current run through the metal — fine on a 1960s cast iron, dangerous on anything modern, illegal on plastic.

  • Don't wait three days.

    The longer the line sits frozen, the further the freeze spreads outward and the more likely a joint cracks from ice expansion. Call the morning it starts.

After the thaw

A line that froze once will freeze again — unless we find out why.

The thaw is the first thing. The second thing is figuring out why the freeze happened where it did, because if the cause is structural — a belly, a back-pitched section, a partial collapse — it will freeze again next cold snap. We run a half-price camera the same day and show you the spot.

Frozen sewer FAQ

Questions homeowners ask at −30°C.

How do I know it's actually frozen, and not just a clog?
Two signs together: every drain in the house is dead at once (a regular clog is usually one branch line), and a cable run can't get past the property-line area — it hits something hard and slick and slides back. Time of year matters too. If it's January, the house had a cold night, and you've got both symptoms, it's a freeze. We'll confirm on site before charging.
How long does a thaw take?
Most residential thaws are between one and three hours from set-up to clear-flow. A short freeze (10 to 20 feet inside the foundation) can be done in under an hour. A long deep freeze that runs all the way to the city main can be four to six hours in extreme cases. We'll give you a time estimate as soon as we know how far the plug is, usually within the first 15 minutes.
Is steam thawing safe on my pipe?
Yes. Steam at 212°F is the same temperature as boiling water, just delivered through an insulated rod inside the pipe. It's safe on clay tile, cast iron, PVC, ABS, lead, concrete, and Orangeburg. We've never thawed a line we damaged. The dangerous methods — electric arc and open flame — we don't run.
Will my line freeze again next winter?
If the reason it froze is just an unusually cold night and standing water in a normally fine line, probably not — but it depends on the cause. About 60% of the frozen lines we thaw have an underlying problem (a belly, a partial root mass, a low-pitched section). That's why we recommend a same-day camera scope at half price — so you know whether to expect this every January or it was a one-off.
Can the City of Winnipeg help cover the cost?
The City runs a frozen-water-service program in extreme-cold winters that covers frozen incoming water supply lines, not frozen sewers. Frozen sanitary sewers between your house and the city main are the homeowner's responsibility. That said, ask your insurance — some Manitoba sewer-backup policies cover thaw costs after a backup incident. We provide an itemized invoice for the claim.
What about the city main? Whose side is the freeze on?
The dividing line is the property line — sometimes the curb, sometimes a foot or two onto the boulevard. From your basement out to that line is your responsibility. From there to the city main is the City's. If we thaw and the rod goes 90 feet without finding water, it usually means the freeze is past your property line and we'll help you open a ticket with 311 — they roll their own crew.
Can I get a thaw at 11 p.m.?
Yes. We run an after-hours line. Frozen sewer with a houseful of people is the kind of call we open the bay door for at midnight. There's no after-hours surcharge — the rate is the same as a Tuesday afternoon. If we're already on another emergency, we'll give you a real ETA and tell you what to do in the meantime.
Do you handle frozen drains inside the building, like a floor drain or laundry standpipe?
Yes — those are usually faster jobs. A standpipe or floor drain that froze near a poorly-insulated wall is often thawed in 20 to 40 minutes with a portable steamer or a hot-water flush. Same flat starting rate, same no-after-hours-surcharge policy.
