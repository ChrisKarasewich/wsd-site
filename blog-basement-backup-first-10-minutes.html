What to do in the first 10 minutes of a basement backup.

Five things to do before we arrive — and one thing not to do. Shutting off the right fixture can be the difference between a $400 clear and a $4,000 remediation.

MS
M. Strachan
Dispatcher · Winnipeg Sewer & Drain

Most calls to the dispatcher start the same way: "There's water coming up the floor drain." Then the homeowner asks how fast we can be there. The honest answer is 60 to 90 minutes — and the next ten minutes, before we arrive, decide whether this is a $400 clear or a $4,000 remediation job.

Here's the playbook we read out over the phone. Five things to do, in order. One thing not to do.

1. Stop running water in the house.

This is the single biggest thing. Whatever's backing up — sewer line, floor drain, laundry standpipe — every fixture upstream of the blockage is making it worse. Tell everyone to stop washing, flushing, and showering. If the dishwasher or washing machine is mid-cycle, hit cancel and let it pause. You can finish that load tomorrow.

2. Find the lowest fixture in the house.

The lowest drain is where everything comes out first — usually the basement floor drain or the basement shower. Knowing which fixture it is tells our tech a lot before we even open the truck. It also tells you which drain to put a towel ring around (next step).

If you have a sewer backup valve
About 30% of newer Winnipeg homes have one. It's the brass cover with a square nut on top of the floor drain, or the inline check valve in the basement ceiling. If you know where yours is, this is the moment to confirm it's seated. Don't force it — just check it's not held open.

3. Build a containment ring.

Whatever's coming up is going to keep coming up, slowly, until pressure equalizes. A ring of old towels, a roll of paper towel, or one of those plumber's putty rings from the hardware store keeps the spread from doubling every five minutes. Don't try to seal it — sealing it forces the water somewhere worse. Just slow the spread.

4. Move what you can.

Cardboard boxes wick water faster than anything in a basement. Lift any cardboard off the floor. Same for rugs, anything fabric-backed, and the bottom row of anything on metal shelving. You have ten minutes — don't try to empty the room. Pick the things that can't be replaced or that will spread the damage if they soak through.

5. Take three photos.

One wide shot of the room, one close-up of the active drain, one of any visible water line on the wall. If you end up filing an insurance claim — and on a real backup, you should — these three photos are what the adjuster will ask for first. Take them on your phone, with the timestamp on. We can email you our camera footage to add to the file once we've run the line.

The one thing not to do.

Don't pour drain cleaner down the line.

We get this call once a week. Someone hears the gurgle, pours a bottle of liquid drain cleaner into the floor drain, and now there's standing chemical water in the basement and our tech has to suit up to work on it. The cleaner doesn't reach the actual blockage — it's a sewer line problem, not a hair clog — and it makes the next two hours noticeably worse for everyone.

Then call us.

Our after-hours line is open 24/7. If you've got an active backup, we hold same-day slots and roll inside 90 minutes most of the time. You don't pay a service-call fee, you don't pay a diagnostic if we end up doing the work, and the quote is written down before the cable hits the line.
