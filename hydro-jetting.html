When you need it

Cable opens the line. Jetting cleans it.

A sectional cable cuts a channel through a clog — fast relief, but the pipe wall is still coated in everything that built up before. The next clog starts forming the moment you turn the water back on.

Hydro jetting fires water at the inside of the pipe at 3,500–4,000 PSI. It strips grease, scale, sediment, and soft root mats off the wall — leaving the line at close to its original 4-inch diameter. Months between clogs, not weeks.

Anatomy

How a jet nozzle actually cleans.

PUSH, THEN PULL

The nozzle is fed to the far end of the line first. Cleaning happens on the slow pull back, so debris flushes out the way it came in.

REAR-FIRING JETS DO THE WORK

85% of the water pressure is aimed backward — pulling the nozzle forward and scouring the pipe wall behind it. Only 15% goes forward.

CAMERA BEFORE AND AFTER

We scope the line before the jet to confirm it's a candidate, and after to show you a clean wall. Footage emailed same day.

Equipment

Different blockage, different nozzle.

Penetrator

Standard wash.

Three rear jets, one forward. The everyday nozzle for grease, sludge, and scale in a line that's already mostly open. Goes fast.

Root cutter

Soft roots, hard scale.

Rear jets plus a forward chisel jet that cuts soft root mats and chips off mineral scale. Used when the camera shows hairline root intrusion.

Chain flail

Hardened scale, descaling cast iron.

Chains spin on a nozzle head, mechanically descaling cast iron drain lines back to bare metal. The right tool for 1960s-era pipe.

What jetting actually cuts through

What 4,000 PSI of water cleans — and what it can't.

YES

Cooking grease and soap scum.

The single biggest reason kitchen lines clog. Cable cuts a hole in it; jet water dissolves and flushes it. Restaurants on a scheduled jetting plan see fewer than one emergency call a year.

YES

Mineral scale.

The hard, chalky buildup on the inside of older cast-iron lines. A chain-flail nozzle scours it off mechanically. The pipe walls come out looking like new metal.

YES

Soft root intrusion.

Hair-thin roots growing through joint seals in clay tile. The jet cuts them, scours the joint, and we follow with a root-control foam treatment if the line is otherwise sound.

YES

Sediment, sand, and rocks.

Common in older catch basins, foundation drains, and sump discharge lines. The jet pushes it back to the cleanout where we vacuum it out.

NO

Mature, woody root masses.

Thick rope-like roots in a 4-inch lateral need mechanical cutting first — a sectional cable with a root-cutter head, not water. We jet after, to clean what the cable left behind.

NO

Broken pipe.

If the camera shows a cracked, offset, or collapsed pipe, jetting won't help and can make it worse by washing soil into the cavity. You need a spot repair or liner. We'll tell you on the spot.

Hydro jetting FAQ

Questions on this service.

Won't 4,000 PSI of water blow my pipe apart?
No — and the camera makes sure of it. Modern PVC, cast iron, and ABS handle 4,000 PSI without issue. Older clay tile and Orangeburg can be vulnerable if it's already cracked, which is exactly what the camera tells us before the nozzle goes in. If the line isn't a jetting candidate, we tell you and use a different method.
How is jetting different from cabling or snaking?
A cable cuts a channel through a clog. Water keeps flowing, but the pipe wall is still coated with grease, scale, and sediment — so the next clog forms quickly. Jetting strips the pipe wall back to its original diameter. For a clog you've cleared three times in a year, jet it once and you usually get a year or more before the next one.
How long does a typical jetting job take?
Camera plus jet on a 50–80 foot residential line runs about 90 minutes to 2.5 hours. Restaurant kitchen mains usually run two to four hours depending on length and grease severity.
Does the jet make a mess?
Done right, no. We jet from a downstream cleanout so debris flushes back the way the nozzle came in — into the cleanout, where we vacuum it out. We lay tarps on hard flooring and bring shoe covers. The truck handles the wastewater; nothing goes into your yard.
Will jetting fix a broken pipe?
No. If the camera shows broken, offset, or collapsed pipe, jetting won't help — and high-pressure water through a hole will wash soil into the cavity and make repairs worse. We stop, show you the footage, and quote a spot repair or liner instead. Your camera fee applies to the repair.
How often should a line be jetted?
Residential kitchen lines with heavy use: every 18–24 months. Cast iron mains over 50 years old: every 12–24 months. Restaurant kitchen mains: every 3–6 months. We tell you a recommended interval after the first job, based on what the camera shows.
Do I need a cleanout for you to jet?
Strongly preferred. A proper main cleanout gives us straight-line access and contains debris flush-back. If you don't have one, we can sometimes jet through a roof vent or pulled toilet, but it's slower and there's a reset fee. We can quote installing a cleanout — usually pays for itself the second time the line needs work.
Can hydro jetting damage tree roots in the line permanently?
It cuts soft roots cleanly, but the roots themselves are growing outside the pipe and will come back. The lasting fix is either a root-control foam treatment after the jet (slows regrowth by 1–3 years), or fixing the joint the roots came through — typically a spot repair or pipe liner. We'll lay out the options after the camera.
