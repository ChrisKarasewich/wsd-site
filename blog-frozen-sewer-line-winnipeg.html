Frozen sewer line in Winnipeg: what to do, what not to do, and how to prevent it.

A frozen sewer line stops every drain in the house. Here is exactly what to do in the moment, what to never do, and how to keep it from happening again next winter.

MS
M. Strachan
Dispatcher · Winnipeg Sewer & Drain

Winnipeg winters are hard on sewer lines. Frost can reach 8 feet or deeper in a cold January. If your line runs shallow, or a section is exposed to cold air, you can wake up to a frozen line and a house where nothing drains.

Here's what to do in the moment, and how to keep it from happening again.

The short version
A frozen sewer line stops your home's drains from working. Run hot water through the system, but do not pour hot water directly into the drain, do not use a torch or open flame near the pipe, and call a professional with proper thawing equipment. To prevent it next winter: keep the line in use, insulate exposed sections, and get a fall camera inspection.

How to tell if your sewer line is frozen

The symptoms look like a clog, but with a few tells:

  • All drains back up at the same time — not just one fixture
  • Toilets gurgle but don't flush properly
  • The basement floor drain is the first to show water
  • It started during a deep cold snap (−30°C or colder for several days)
  • A foul smell from the drains as backup water sits

If only one drain is affected, you have a clog. If every drain is affected and the weather has been brutal, freezing is the likely cause.

What to do right now

  • Stop using water in the house. Every flush, shower, and sink puts more water into a line that has nowhere to send it. The water backs up.
  • Run the hot water tap upstream of the freeze. A slow trickle of warm water can melt the start of an ice block — works best for partial freezes caught early.
  • Call a professional. A frozen line needs a proper thawing rig that pushes warm water or steam through the pipe to melt the blockage from the inside.

WSD runs sewer thawing as a core service through Winnipeg winters — same-day response across the city, and a camera inspection after the thaw to make sure the line wasn't damaged by the ice.

What NOT to do
Do not use a torch, open flame, or heat gun on the pipe — that can crack it, or start a fire in a wall cavity. Do not pour boiling water down the drain — the sudden temperature change can crack ABS or cast iron. Do not pour chemical cleaner into a frozen line — it sits on the ice, eats the pipe, and sprays back when it thaws. Do not keep flushing toilets hoping to clear it — you'll overflow the system inside the house.

How sewer thawing works

A thawing rig pushes hot water (or sometimes steam) through a small hose run into the line. The hose advances as the ice melts. Once the line is open, a camera inspection confirms the pipe wasn't damaged. A typical thaw takes anywhere from under an hour to a few hours, depending on how far down the freeze is and how solid the ice block is.

How to prevent a frozen line next winter

  • Keep the line in use. Lines with regular flow rarely freeze. The risk is highest at cottages, vacant homes, or rarely-used basement bathrooms. If a property will sit empty, drain the line.
  • Insulate exposed sections. Pipe through a cold crawl space, unheated garage, or against an exterior wall is the most common freeze point. Pipe insulation and heat tape are cheap fixes.
  • Check the depth of your line. Older homes sometimes have lines that don't meet modern frost-depth standards. A camera inspection can map the depth.
  • Get a fall camera inspection. A line with cracks, root damage, or poor slope freezes faster. A camera before winter catches problems early.
  • Be careful with snow removal. Snow over a cleanout actually insulates it. Bare ground freezes deeper. Counterintuitive, but true.

Frequently asked questions

At what temperature does a sewer line freeze in Winnipeg?

There's no single temperature. Frost penetration depends on how long the cold lasts, how much snow cover is on the ground, and how deep the line is buried. Most freezes happen during sustained cold snaps below −25°C for several days.

Can a frozen sewer line burst?

Yes. Water expands when it freezes, and ice can crack the pipe — especially older clay tile or cast iron. After a thaw, a camera inspection checks for damage.

Is sewer thawing covered by insurance?

The thawing itself usually isn't. Damage from a backup caused by the freeze sometimes is, if you have sewer-backup coverage. Check with your provider.

How long does it take to thaw a frozen sewer line?

Most thaws take from under an hour to a few hours. Deeper freezes and longer ice blocks take longer.

Why does my sewer line freeze every winter?

Recurring freezes usually mean the line is too shallow, has poor slope, or runs through an unheated space. A camera inspection finds the cause; the fix depends on what it shows.
