Why does my drain keep clogging? 6 common causes (and how to fix them for good).

A drain that clogs once is a one-time problem. A drain that clogs every few months is a symptom of something deeper in the line. Here are the six causes we see most — and what actually fixes each.

MS
M. Strachan
Dispatcher · Winnipeg Sewer & Drain

You pour the drain cleaner in. You snake it. The water runs fine for a few weeks. Then the sink is full again.

If that sounds familiar, the clog isn't really the problem. The problem is whatever keeps causing the clog. Here are the six causes we see most often in Winnipeg homes, and what actually fixes each one.

The short version
A drain that clogs once is a one-time problem. A drain that clogs every few months is a symptom of something deeper. The six usual suspects: grease buildup, hair, foreign objects, tree roots, pipe damage, and a clog further down the main line. The only way to know for sure is a camera inspection.

1. Grease and soap buildup

Every time hot grease, cooking oil, or soapy water goes down the drain, a thin film coats the inside of the pipe and hardens as it cools. Over months, the pipe narrows until even a little food or hair gets stuck.

A standard snake punches a hole through the buildup. Water flows again — but the grease layer is still there, and it traps the next thing that comes down. The fix: hydro jetting. High-pressure water scours the pipe clean. Not a hole through the buildup — the buildup gone.

2. Hair (in bathroom drains)

Hair binds with soap scum into a rope that grips the inside of the pipe. Snaking pulls some of it out; the rest stays. The fix: a proper cleaning that reaches past the trap, and a hair catcher on the drain going forward. Cheap, simple, works.

3. Foreign objects

Kids' toys, dental floss, wipes labelled "flushable" (they aren't), cotton swabs, the lid of a shampoo bottle. Anything that doesn't dissolve can wedge into a bend and catch everything after it. The fix: a camera finds the object, then it gets removed. No amount of liquid cleaner will help.

4. Tree roots in the line

Common in older Winnipeg neighbourhoods. Roots seek water, and clay tile sewer lines (standard before the 1960s) have joints every few feet. Roots find those joints and grow inside the pipe. A snake cuts through them and clears the line for a while — then they grow back.

The fix: mechanical root cutting clears the line, hydro jetting cleans it, and a camera confirms how bad the damage is. If the pipe itself is failing, a repair or replacement is the long-term answer. More on this in our piece on tree roots in your sewer line.

5. Damaged or collapsed pipe

Pipes shift, crack, corrode. Old cast iron rusts from the inside out; clay tile breaks; concrete corrodes. When the pipe itself is damaged, debris catches on the rough edges and clogs the line over and over. The fix: a camera shows exactly where the damage is and how bad. Repair options depend on what it finds.

6. A clog deeper in the main sewer line

If multiple drains back up at the same time — kitchen sink, basement floor drain, toilet — the clog isn't in one drain. It's in the main line that runs from the house to the street. The fix: a main line cleaning, with a camera before and after so you know what caused it and whether the line is healthy.

Frequently asked questions

Why does my drain keep clogging even after I snake it?

Snaking punches a hole through the clog. The cause — grease, roots, a damaged pipe — is still there. Within weeks or months, the line fills in again. To fix it for good, you have to find and clear the cause, not the symptom.

Is liquid drain cleaner safe to use?

Chemical cleaners are hard on pipes, especially older ones. They corrode metal and weaken plastic, and they rarely solve recurring clogs. We don't recommend them.

How often should a sewer line be cleaned?

For most Winnipeg homes, every 2 to 3 years. Homes with mature trees, or clay tile lines, may need it more often. A camera inspection tells you when.

What's the difference between drain cleaning and hydro jetting?

Drain cleaning (snaking) clears a clog. Hydro jetting cleans the pipe. Both have their place — for grease and recurring clogs, jetting is the better choice.
