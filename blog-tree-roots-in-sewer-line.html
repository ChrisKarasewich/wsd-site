Tree roots in your sewer line: signs, causes, and what to actually do.

Roots get in through cracks and joints, grow inside the pipe, and cause recurring clogs. The fix is mechanical cutting, hydro jetting, and a camera to see how bad the damage really is. Removing the tree rarely helps.

JR
J. Reimer
Lead tech · Winnipeg Sewer & Drain

Winnipeg has a lot of mature trees. Elm, ash, maple, poplar. Older neighbourhoods — Wolseley, Crescentwood, Norwood, River Heights — are full of them. Those same neighbourhoods are also full of clay tile sewer lines with joints every few feet.

Trees love water. Sewer lines have water. The math is straightforward.

The short version
Roots get into sewer lines through small cracks and joints, grow inside the pipe, and cause recurring clogs. The fix is mechanical root cutting, hydro jetting to clean the line, and a camera to see how damaged the pipe really is. Removing the tree rarely helps — the long-term fix is repairing the section the roots are getting into.

How roots get into a sewer line

A line doesn't have to be broken for roots to find their way in. Three openings are usually enough.

  • Pipe joints. Older clay tile lines have a joint every 3 feet. Even a perfect joint has a tiny gap. Roots find it, push in, and grow.
  • Cracks. Settling soil, shifting frost, age, and corrosion all crack sewer pipes over time. A crack is an open door.
  • Failed seals. Where the line meets the foundation, or where two materials join, the seal can fail. Same result.

Once a root is inside, it grows fast — the line is full of nutrients and water. A small root can become a pipe-blocking mass in a single growing season.

Signs you have roots in your line

  • Slow drains across the whole house — not one fixture, the whole system
  • Gurgling toilets when other drains run
  • A foul smell in the basement or yard
  • Sinkholes or soft spots in the lawn above the line
  • Recurring clogs that come back every few months, no matter how often you snake them

The recurring part is the giveaway. A one-time clog is a one-time clog. A clog that comes back is a sign of something growing.

What to do if you have roots

Three steps, in order.

1. Camera inspection. A camera in the line shows exactly where the roots are, how much damage they've done, and the condition of the pipe. Without this, every other step is a guess.

2. Root removal. A mechanical cutter — a rotating blade run through the line — cuts the roots out. Hydro jetting follows to flush the debris and clean the inside of the pipe.

3. Address the pipe. This is the step most plumbers skip. Cutting the roots gets the line working, but they come back — sometimes in months. The only long-term fix is dealing with the opening they're using:

  • Minor damage: a chemical root inhibitor (a foam that coats the pipe) slows regrowth for one to two years.
  • Moderate damage: a spot repair or relining of the affected section seals the opening permanently.
  • Severe damage: a full replacement of the affected line section.

Does removing the tree help?

Almost never. By the time roots are in your sewer line, the tree's root network is far larger than the visible canopy. Cutting the tree down leaves the roots behind, where they keep growing toward the water source for years.

How to keep roots from getting back in

  • Get an annual camera inspection if you have mature trees near the line. Early detection is cheap; late detection is not.
  • Use a root inhibitor on a schedule — every 12 to 24 months for most properties.
  • Repair the line. If the camera shows a damaged section, fix it before it gets worse. Roots get bigger every year they're left alone.

Frequently asked questions

How do I know if I have tree roots versus a regular clog?

A regular clog usually affects one fixture and clears with snaking. Root clogs affect multiple drains, come back every few months, and often cause gurgling or slow drains across the whole house. A camera inspection confirms it.

Can I dissolve tree roots with chemicals?

Copper sulfate and foaming root killers can slow growth, but they don't dissolve a root mass that's already blocking the line. Mechanical removal first, chemical inhibitor for maintenance.

Will my insurance cover root damage to my sewer line?

Usually not. Most standard policies exclude damage from tree roots or gradual wear. Some sewer-backup endorsements cover cleanup if the backup happened in the home. Check with your insurance provider.

How often should I clean my line if I have big trees in the yard?

For most Winnipeg properties with mature trees, every 2 to 3 years — sometimes annually. A camera inspection tells you whether you need it more or less often.

Can roots be removed without digging up my yard?

Yes, in most cases. Mechanical cutting and hydro jetting are done through an existing cleanout — no digging. Digging only comes into play if the pipe itself needs repair.
